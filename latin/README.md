## Introduction to Latin
Latin (Lingua Latina) is a classical language belonging to the Italic branch of the Indo-European languages. Originally spoken in the area around Rome, it became the dominant language of the Roman Empire. It is the ancestor of the modern Romance languages (French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian) and heavily influenced English vocabulary.

## Alphabet & Pronunciation
The Latin alphabet is the basis of the modern English alphabet. Classical Latin lacked J, U, and W (using I and V instead).
- **C:** Always hard like 'k' in "cat" (never soft like "cell").
- **G:** Always hard like 'g' in "go".
- **V:** Pronounced like an English 'w' (e.g., veni = "weh-nee").
- **Ecclesiastical vs. Classical:** Two main pronunciation systems exist. Classical aims for historical accuracy; Ecclesiastical is used by the Catholic Church.

## Grammar Overview
Latin is a highly inflected language.
- **Cases:** Six primary cases (Nominative, Genitive, Dative, Accusative, Ablative, Vocative) dictating noun function.
- **Word Order:** Generally flexible due to the case system, but Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) is most common.
- **Conjugations and Declensions:** Verbs belong to conjugations; nouns belong to declensions.
