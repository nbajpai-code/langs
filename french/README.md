## Introduction to French
French (Français) is a Romance language of the Indo-European family. It descended from the Vulgar Latin of the Roman Empire, as did all Romance languages. French is an official language in 29 countries across multiple continents, most notably in France, Canada, Belgium, Switzerland, and many nations in Western and Central Africa.

## Alphabet & Pronunciation
The French alphabet is based on the Latin alphabet and consists of 26 letters, just like English, but with several diacritics (accent marks) that affect pronunciation:
- **L'accent aigu (é):** Only on 'e', pronounced like the "ay" in "say".
- **L'accent grave (à, è, ù):** Changes the pronunciation of 'e' to a short "eh" sound, and distinguishes words that are otherwise homographs (e.g., *ou* vs *où*).
- **L'accent circonflexe (â, ê, î, ô, û):** Often indicates that an 's' used to follow the vowel in Old French (e.g., *hôpital*).
- **La cédille (ç):** Makes a 'c' sound like an 's' before 'a', 'o', or 'u'.
- **Le tréma (ë, ï, ü):** Indicates that two adjacent vowels should be pronounced separately.

**Key Pronunciation Rules:**
- **Silent Letters:** Final consonants (D, P, S, T, X, Z) are usually silent, except in cases of *liaison* (linking to the next word starting with a vowel).
- **H:** Almost always silent.
- **R:** A guttural sound produced in the back of the throat.
- **Nasal Vowels:** Vowels followed by 'n' or 'm' are often nasalized (e.g., *bon*, *vin*, *un*).

## Grammar Overview
French grammar shares many traits with other Romance languages:
- **Gendered Nouns:** All nouns are either masculine (often using *le*/*un*) or feminine (often using *la*/*une*).
- **Adjective Placement:** Most adjectives come *after* the noun they modify, but a specific group of common adjectives (Beauty, Age, Number, Goodness, Size - BANGS) come *before* the noun.
- **Subject Pronouns:** Unlike Spanish, French requires the subject pronoun (e.g., *Je parle français*).
- **Verb Conjugation:** Verbs are conjugated based on subject, tense, and mood. There are three main regular verb groups ending in: **-er**, **-ir**, and **-re**.
- **Tu vs. Vous:** *Tu* is the informal/singular "you," while *Vous* is the formal/plural "you."

## Learning Resources

### Textbooks & Courses
- **Practice Makes Perfect: Complete French Grammar**: Excellent for drilling grammar rules and vocabulary.
- **Assimil French**: A classic course focusing on reading and listening to bilingual dialogues for natural acquisition.
- **Michel Thomas Method (French)**: An audio-only course that builds sentences from the ground up, focusing on grammatical structure without memorization.
- **Pimsleur French**: Great audio course for improving accent and conversational confidence.

### Online Platforms & Apps
- **Duolingo / Memrise**: Good starting points for vocabulary building and gamified daily practice.
- **Babbel**: Provides a more structured, practical approach to learning grammar and conversational phrases.
- **Kwiziq / Lawless French**: Incredible resources for taking localized grammar tests and reading deep-dive explanations of French grammar nuances.
- **Italki**: Best platform for finding French tutors and conversation partners for speaking practice.

### Dictionaries
- **WordReference**: Excellent for bilingual dictionary needs, complete with conjugations and helpful forums for idiomatic expressions.
- **Linguee**: Great for seeing words and phrases translated in context using bilingual texts.
- **Larousse**: The standard dictionary for French; highly recommended for intermediate and advanced learners (monolingual options).

### Media & Listening Practice
- **Podcasts**:
  - *Coffee Break French*: Perfect for beginners moving through conversational basics.
  - *InnerFrench*: Phenomenal resource for intermediate learners; Hugo speaks slowly and clearly about interesting topics.
  - *Français Authentique*: Great for upper-intermediate learners wanting natural expressions and vocabulary.
- **YouTube**:
  - *French Mornings with Elisa*: Engaging videos explaining grammar and expressions.
  - *Easy French*: Street interviews showing real, spoken French with dual subtitles.
- **Netflix / Streaming**:
  - Watch series like *Lupin*, *Dix pour cent (Call My Agent!)*, or *Le Bureau des Légendes* to immerse yourself in the sound and culture of modern French.
