## Introduction to Arabic
Arabic (العربية, al-ʻarabiyyah) is a Central Semitic language that first emerged in Iron Age northwestern Arabia. It is the lingua franca of the Arab world and the liturgical language of Islam. Arabic is typically divided into Modern Standard Arabic (MSA), used in writing and formal speech, and various regional dialects (Ammiya), used in everyday conversation.

## Alphabet & Pronunciation
The Arabic alphabet is written from right to left in a cursive style and contains 28 letters.
- **Abjad:** It is an impure abjad; short vowels are not typically written out but can be indicated by diacritics (tashkeel) above or below the consonants.
- **Letter Forms:** Letters change shape depending on their position in a word (isolated, initial, medial, final).
- **Phonology:** Features "emphatic" consonants and guttural sounds produced deep in the throat (e.g., 'ayn, ghayn, qaf).

## Grammar Overview
- **Root System:** Like other Semitic languages, Arabic words are derived from 3-letter (triconsonantal) roots conveying a basic meaning, modified by applying vowel patterns and affixes.
- **Word Order:** Classically Verb-Subject-Object (VSO), though Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) is also common in MSA and dialects.
- **Gender:** Masculine and Feminine. Nouns, adjectives, and verbs must agree in gender.
- **Dual:** Features a specific dual number form for referring to exactly two items, distinct from singular and plural.
